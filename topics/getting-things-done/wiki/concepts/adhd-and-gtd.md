ADHD and GTD connect around externalized attention management: GTD turns loose commitments, reminders, and ideas into visible state that can be reviewed outside working memory. The fit is practical rather than clinical; GTD can support focus and organization, but it does not replace medical or mental-health care.

## Core Fit

The strongest overlap is with the [[trusted-system]] idea. If attention is fragmented or working memory is unreliable, the GTD system becomes a stable place to park open loops until they can be clarified, reviewed, and acted on.

Useful GTD elements for ADHD include:

- low-friction capture for thoughts before they disappear
- [[next-actions]] that remove hidden planning from execution
- [[review]] as a reset loop when the system has gone stale
- [[someday-maybe]] as a pressure valve for high idea volume
- clear separation between active commitments and possible future work

## Failure Modes

GTD can become counterproductive for ADHD when the system grows too complex, review is skipped, or every idea is promoted into an active commitment. The system should stay small enough to use on low-energy days.

## Practical Rule

Start with one inbox, one next-action list, one someday/maybe list, and one review checklist. Add structure only when repeated review shows a real need.
